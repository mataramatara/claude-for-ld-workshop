Learner Persona

A detailed profile of a primary learner segment. Drop this into your Claude Project so every output is designed for the right person.

M
Marcus, 34
First-Year People Manager · Operations

Promoted from individual contributor eight months ago. Managing a team of five for the first time. Technically strong; still building his people skills and manager identity.

First-time manager High performer → new role Time-scarce Self-taught learner Skeptical of "soft skills" training

Background & Context

Marcus was promoted because he was excellent at the job — not because he'd demonstrated strong management instincts. He stepped into the role with confidence in his technical judgment and real uncertainty about everything else: giving feedback, navigating conflict, running 1:1s, advocating for his team upward.

He's nine months in. The initial learning curve was steep and mostly self-directed — books he found, YouTube videos, peers he asked. He's learned a lot, but the learning has been uneven. He's strong at the tactical (setting up workflows, tracking performance data) and shakier on the relational (difficult conversations, recognition, coaching through mistakes).

He works in operations, which means his team is measured daily. He feels the pressure acutely. When something goes wrong with a team member, the instinct is to solve the problem himself rather than coach the person through it.

What Marcus Sounds Like

"I know I'm supposed to coach, not just tell people what to do. But honestly, I tell them and it gets done faster. I don't have time to do the Socratic method every time there's a fire." — Marcus, in a focus group on manager development
"The training I've done so far felt like it was designed for someone in a different job. I need specifics, not frameworks." — Marcus, in a post-session survey

Goals & Motivations

What Marcus wants

  • To be respected by his team — not just liked
  • To get results without burning people out (including himself)
  • To feel less reactive and more in control as a manager
  • To handle the hard conversations without dreading them for three days first
  • To understand what good management actually looks like in his context, not a generic one

What Marcus doesn't want

  • Theoretical frameworks he has to "translate" to his real work
  • Role-plays that feel staged and nothing like his actual team
  • Content that assumes he has unlimited time
  • To feel like a bad manager in the learning environment
  • More information — he has plenty. He wants practice and feedback.

Prior Knowledge & Skill Level

Strengths he brings

  • Deep subject-matter expertise in operations — credible with his team
  • Strong analytical thinking — good at diagnosing process problems
  • Self-awareness about his gaps — he knows what he's not good at yet
  • Some exposure to feedback models (SBI) from a previous workshop — needs practice, not re-teaching
  • High motivation to grow — this job matters to him

Gaps and challenges

  • Avoids conflict by over-explaining or solving the problem himself
  • Gives positive feedback awkwardly — tends to underdo it
  • Struggles to coach without directing — defaults to "here's what I would do"
  • Hasn't built the habit of regular 1:1s — cancels them when things get busy
  • Doesn't know how to have a "performance is below expectations" conversation — has never done one

Learning Preferences & Behaviors

  • Short over long. He will not read a 40-page guide. He will read a 2-page job aid that tells him exactly what to do in a specific situation.
  • Examples over principles. "Show me a real conversation that went well and one that didn't. Let me see the difference."
  • Learns by doing, not watching. Demonstration is useful; practice is necessary. He needs reps, not lectures.
  • Mobile and on-the-go. More likely to review content on his phone between meetings than sit down at a desk.
  • Skeptical of "soft skills" framing. Frame learning outcomes in terms of results and team performance — not feelings or interpersonal harmony.
  • Needs psychological safety to practice hard things. Won't engage authentically in practice if he thinks he'll be judged for getting it wrong.

Technology & Access

  • Uses a company laptop and personal iPhone — content needs to work on both
  • No dedicated learning time built into his schedule — all learning competes with meetings
  • Has access to the LMS but hasn't logged in since onboarding — doesn't see it as a resource
  • Comfortable with video, interactive tools, and anything that feels more like an app than a course

Design Implications — What This Means for Content

  • Lead with the situation, not the concept. Marcus responds to "here's what to do when your team member misses a deadline for the third time" more than "here's the feedback model."
  • Keep sessions under 20 minutes or design for flexible consumption in chunks.
  • Build in practice with realistic scenarios — name the scenarios after real operations situations, not generic "Employee A" placeholders.
  • Outcome language matters. "You'll be able to have a performance conversation" lands better than "you'll understand how to have a performance conversation."
  • Don't retrain what he already knows. Acknowledge SBI, build on it, don't re-explain it.
  • Design for graceful failure. If he does the wrong thing in a scenario, the consequence should be instructive, not punishing.
