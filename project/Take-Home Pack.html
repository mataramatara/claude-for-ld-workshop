Part One · 12 promptsThe L&D Prompt Library.

Copy a prompt. Replace the bracketed parts. Run it. Iterate. Each prompt names the job it's for and why it works.

Design

01 — Learning objectives from a messy transcript

Best for: SME interviews · Output: 3-6 objectives
Act as a senior instructional designer. I'll paste a transcript of an SME interview about [TOPIC]. Generate 3–6 learning objectives written at the appropriate Bloom's level for [AUDIENCE: new hires / managers / etc]. For each objective: state the verb, the content, and the condition. Then briefly flag which parts of the transcript support it. Transcript: [PASTE TRANSCRIPT]

Why it works: forces Claude to ground the objectives in source evidence instead of generating generic ones.

02 — Workshop outline from an objective

Best for: instructor-led design · Output: timed table
Design a [DURATION]-minute in-person workshop that achieves this objective: [PASTE OBJECTIVE]. Audience: [AUDIENCE DESCRIPTION + SIZE]. Output a table with columns: time, activity, facilitator action, learner action, and assessment moment. Include at least one activity that surfaces prior experience and one that practices the new behavior. Ask me 3 clarifying questions before you draft.

Why it works: the clarifying-question step prevents generic outputs.

03 — Convert content to a scenario

Best for: moving from lecture to application
Take the policy/procedure below and turn it into a realistic workplace scenario for [AUDIENCE]. The scenario should: - Introduce a dilemma, not just a demonstration - Include 2 distractor details that feel real but aren't decision-critical - End with a decision point, not a conclusion Source: [PASTE POLICY]
Assess

04 — Scenario-based multiple choice

Best for: knowledge + application checks
Generate [N] scenario-based multiple-choice items on [TOPIC] for [AUDIENCE]. Format per item: - Stem: a 2–3 sentence realistic scenario - 4 options (A–D), one correct - A 1-sentence rationale for why each distractor is plausible - The Bloom's level you targeted Avoid trivia. Every item should require judgment.

Why it works: "plausible distractor rationale" is the thing that stops lazy MCQs.

05 — Critique my assessment

Best for: polishing existing items
Here's a set of assessment items I drafted. Critique them as a skeptical assessment specialist would. For each item call out: - Does it test what it claims to test? - Is the stem clear, or does it test reading comprehension? - Are distractors genuinely plausible for a novice? - Any cultural, demographic, or context bias? Be specific. Then rewrite the weakest 3 items. Items: [PASTE ITEMS]
Deliver

06 — Facilitator script with timing cues

Best for: handoff to other facilitators
Turn the attached outline into a facilitator script for someone delivering this session for the first time. Include: - What the facilitator says (in natural speaking voice) - Timing cues in [brackets] - Watch-outs: where learners commonly get confused - What to do if an activity runs long Outline: [PASTE OUTLINE]

07 — One-page job aid

Best for: reinforcement · Output: printable
Create a one-page job aid for [TASK] that a [ROLE] could keep at their desk. Structure: - Title (6 words max) - 3–5 numbered steps in verb-first voice - A "watch-out" call-out for the most common mistake - An "if X, then Y" decision table for edge cases Optimize for scannability. No paragraphs.

08 — Discussion questions, calibrated

Best for: virtual live, small groups
Generate 6 discussion questions on [TOPIC] calibrated for a [DURATION]-minute small-group conversation. Structure them as: - 2 to surface prior experience (no wrong answers) - 2 to apply a concept to participants' real work - 2 to provoke disagreement For each question, note the Bloom's level and what a facilitator should listen for in the responses.
Analyze

09 — Interrogate a long document

Best for: research, policies, SME docs
I'm attaching a [DOCUMENT TYPE]. Read it carefully. Then, without summarizing yet, answer: 1. What are the 3 most important claims? 2. What assumptions are unstated? 3. Where would a [AUDIENCE] get confused? 4. What 5 questions would I need answered to turn this into a learning experience? [ATTACH / PASTE DOCUMENT]

10 — Compare two frameworks

Best for: competency models, methodologies
I'll paste two [FRAMEWORK TYPE]. Compare them on: - What they agree on - Where they directly conflict - Which audiences each is better suited for - A proposed merged version that preserves the strongest elements of each Framework A: [PASTE A] Framework B: [PASTE B]
Refine

11 — Rewrite in my voice

Best for: maintaining consistency across content
Below are 3 writing samples in my voice and a draft that isn't in my voice. Study the samples, identify the moves I make (sentence length, vocabulary, structure, humor, what I avoid), then rewrite the draft to match. Before rewriting, summarize in 5 bullets what you noticed about my voice. I want to see your work. Samples: [PASTE 3 SAMPLES] Draft to rewrite: [PASTE DRAFT]

Why it works: the "show your work" step catches bad voice-matches before you get a full rewrite.

12 — Executive summary for a specific skeptic

Best for: stakeholder buy-in
Summarize the attached [REPORT / PROPOSAL] for a specific reader: [ROLE + WHAT THEY CARE ABOUT + WHAT THEY'RE SKEPTICAL OF]. Output: 150 words or fewer. Lead with the business outcome. Address the skepticism directly. End with the single decision I'm asking them to make. Then flag: what claims in this summary would you verify before sending? [ATTACH / PASTE CONTENT]

Part Three · Ten habitsSmall moves that compound.

These aren't prompts — they're the posture. Adopt one a week and your outputs will look different in a month without your prompts getting any longer.

01
One task per prompt.

If a prompt is doing three jobs, split it. Focused prompts outperform compound ones — every time.

02
Always add success criteria.

"It's good if X, Y, Z." Claude hits what you name. If you don't name it, you get a guess.

03
Front-load context.

Give everything Claude needs up front. Trickling context costs messages and quality.

04
Stop when it's off track.

Don't keep nudging a drifting conversation. Start a new chat with a cleaner prompt.

05
Ask for the method, not the answer.

"Walk me through how you'd approach this" gets you a better answer than "give me the answer."

06
Tell Claude to push back.

"Disagree with me if I'm wrong. Point out what I'm missing." Its default is agreeable — override that.

07
Test before you share.

Run the prompt yourself before passing it to a teammate. Half the time, you'll edit it first.

08
Don't blindly trust outputs.

Especially financial, regulatory, or citation-level detail. Verify anything a stakeholder will read.

09
Iterate in one-sentence edits.

"Shorter." "More skeptical." "Cut the filler." Small moves beat rewriting the whole prompt.

10
Save what works.

Keep a doc of your ten best prompts. Reuse, don't rewrite. This single habit doubles your output.

Part Five · Claude InstructionsTwo templates. Two scopes.

Claude has two places to store standing instructions: one that applies everywhere, and one scoped to a single Project. Both are worth setting up. Start with Personal Instructions — it's the fastest win.

Personal Instructions Settings → Instructions

Available on all plans. Applies to every chat you open — inside Projects and outside them. Set your defaults once; you won't repeat yourself again.

[YOUR ROLE] I am a ______________ at ______________. [MY AUDIENCE] Most of what I build is for ______________. [MY DEFAULTS] Unless I say otherwise: • Write in plain, direct language — no corporate filler. • Use headers and bullets only when they improve clarity. • Don’t offer unsolicited alternatives. • Keep responses under ______________ unless I ask for more. [MY NON-NEGOTIABLES] • Always flag if you’re uncertain about something. • Never cite a source I haven’t given you. • ______________

Where to find it: Claude.ai → your profile icon → Settings → Instructions

Project Instructions Inside each Project

Scoped to one body of work. Every chat inside this Project inherits these instructions and your uploaded files automatically — no need to re-explain the context.

[ROLE] You are my ______________ for ______________. (e.g., “assessment reviewer for our manager-onboarding redesign”) [AUDIENCE] My learners are ______________. They know ______________ but struggle with ______________. [ALWAYS DO] • Reference [the attached file] before you answer. • ______________ • ______________ [NEVER DO] • Don’t invent statistics or cite sources I haven’t given you. • ______________ [VOICE] Match the tone of this sample paragraph: "[paste 3–5 sentences you wrote — a real email, a program intro, a learner-facing blurb]" [DEFAULT FORMAT] Unless I say otherwise, give me ______________. (e.g., “a table with columns: time · activity · objective · facilitator cue”)

Four places people under-invest

01 · The role

Name the hat. “You are the skeptical SME.” “You are my copy editor.” A named role changes the output more than any other single line.

02 · The voice sample

Paste real writing of yours. 3–5 sentences beats a paragraph of adjectives. If your name goes on it, Claude should sound like you.

03 · The non-negotiables

“Always include an assessment moment.” “Always flag jargon.” House rules, written once, save hours forever.

04 · The files

Drop in your style guide, your competency model, one learner interview. Now every chat in this Project inherits them.

Part Six · The 3-use rule, in practicePrompt vs. Project vs. Skill.

Every time you're about to type the same instructions a third time, you have a choice to make. Here's the decision tree, short and opinionated.

If the work is… Use Why
One-off.
I may never do this again.		 A prompt. No overhead. Type it, use it, close the tab.
Recurring, same context.
One client, one course, one workstream.		 A Project. Files, voice, and instructions stay loaded. Every chat inherits them.
Recurring, any context.
A procedure that applies to many topics.		 A Skill. Lives with Claude. Runs automatically when relevant — in any chat, any Project.
Needs another tool.
Canva, Notion, Google Drive, Slack, etc.		 A Connector. Claude reads from / writes to the tool directly. No copy-paste round-trips.

Skills worth turning on — actual names

These are the names as they appear in the Claude.ai interface. Check under the Skills section — the exact labels may be updated as Anthropic releases new versions.

  1. Create & Export Documents (Word, PowerPoint, PDF). Long-form drafts, formatted reports, polished presentations — exported as actual files you can share or edit.
  2. Analyze Data (Excel/CSV). Drop in survey results or completion data and ask for patterns, summaries, and charts.
  3. Web Search. For anything time-sensitive — regulations, vendor features, research published after Claude's training cutoff.
  4. Claude Designer. The Canva-partnered visual Skill. Lets Claude design directly into your Canva account. (See note below before counting on this one.)
Claude Designer — honest expectations Claude Designer is currently very limited on the free and Pro ($20/month) plans. Full functionality requires a Max plan or higher. Worth exploring — but don't pitch it to your team until you've tested it yourself on your plan.
Community Skills. Skills are also available in shared repositories on GitHub, maintained by the broader Claude community. You can install community-built Skills in your Claude account — but review what each one does before enabling it and handle the permissions carefully. The Anthropic GitHub organization is the safest place to start.
Rule of thumb: Prompts are experiments. Projects are workstreams. Skills are habits. Connectors are bridges. Don’t skip to the bottom of the ladder — climb it as the need appears.

Part Seven · Artifact starter promptsSix things you can build today.

Each of these runs as an Artifact — a real, working deliverable in Claude's side panel. Pick one. Paste the prompt. Swap the brackets. You'll have something in under five minutes.

Before you build — what Claude's Artifacts can and can't do:
Artifacts run in the browser. They handle everything visual: quizzes, drag-and-drops, conversation sims, interactive widgets. What they don't do: save data between sessions, store scores, or connect to your LMS. For that, you'd need a developer or a tool like Airtable or Zapier. If you need it to remember something — that's a different conversation.
01 · Job Aid

One-page reference card

A scannable, printable job aid. Great for process steps, decision guides, or quick-reference tools learners can keep at their desks.

Build a one-page job aid as a clean HTML page. Topic: [TASK OR PROCESS] Audience: [ROLE] Include: a short title, 4–6 numbered steps in verb-first language, one "watch-out" callout, and an if/then decision table for edge cases. Use a clean, professional layout. No long paragraphs — every element scannable.
Connector tip: Add the Canva connector to generate a polished visual version of this job aid directly in your Canva account.
02 · Self-Check

Interactive knowledge check

A browser-based quiz with instant feedback. Learners see whether they got it right and why — no LMS required.

Build an interactive knowledge check as a self-contained HTML page. Topic: [TOPIC] Audience: [LEARNER ROLE AND LEVEL] Include 5 scenario-based multiple-choice questions. Each option should give immediate feedback explaining why it's right or wrong. Show a score at the end. Clean visual design. No frameworks.

Good for: after a module, before a live session, or as a standalone microlearning check.

03 · Widget

Interactive reference tool

A small interactive app — a calculator, a decision guide, a checklist — built around your specific content. More useful than a document; easier than a course.

Build an interactive [CALCULATOR / CHECKLIST / DECISION GUIDE] as a self-contained HTML page. What it does: [DESCRIBE WHAT IT CALCULATES OR HELPS THE USER DECIDE] Audience: [ROLE] Keep the interface simple and mobile-friendly. No frameworks or external dependencies.
Connector tip: Connect Google Drive to pull in your reference doc or data sheet as the content source — Claude reads the file and builds the widget around it.
04 · Simulation

Conversation practice sim

A branching dialogue that lets learners practice a conversation — a feedback conversation, a customer interaction, a coaching moment — with realistic choices and consequences.

Build a conversation practice simulation as an interactive HTML page. Scenario: [DESCRIBE THE SITUATION: who, what's at stake, what the learner needs to do] Include 3 decision points with 3 choices each. Each path should show consequences — what happens next based on what they chose. End with a brief debrief on what worked. No frameworks. Keep it self-contained.
Heads up on sharing: When you share an Artifact link, recipients need a Claude account to view it. For truly standalone delivery — no account required — the HTML file can be hosted on a web server or GitHub Pages.
05 · Microsite

Program or resource hub

A one-page mini-website for a program, resource library, or initiative. Professional-looking enough to share with stakeholders; takes minutes to build.

Build a one-page microsite as a clean HTML file. Program name: [NAME] Purpose: [WHAT THIS PROGRAM DOES] Audience: [WHO IT'S FOR] Sections to include: overview, key outcomes, what to expect, and a contact or next-step section. Warm, professional design. Serif headings. No frameworks or external images.

Good for: program launches, onboarding portals, workshop landing pages.

06 · Game

Learning game or challenge

A simple browser game built around your content — a drag-and-drop matching activity, a timed challenge, a scenario game with a leaderboard moment. More memorable than a quiz.

Build a simple learning game as an interactive HTML page. Game type: [MATCHING / SORTING / TIMED QUIZ / SCENARIO CHALLENGE] Content: [TOPIC OR PASTE THE CONTENT TO BUILD AROUND] Audience: [ROLE] Keep rules simple — learner should understand what to do in under 10 seconds. Show a result at the end. No frameworks.

Good for: reinforcement, team learning moments, engagement boosts.

Part Eight · Work smarterUsing Claude without burning through your plan.

Claude's free plan has generous limits — and a few habits will stretch them further. These aren't restrictions, they're better practices.

01 · The Go Word

At the start of any session, tell Claude: "Don't start building until I say GO. Ask me clarifying questions first."

This prevents Claude from generating a full output before you've given it all the context — which means you won't spend a message correcting a response that was already wrong. Fewer iterations. Better results.

02 · Turn off unused Connectors

Active Connectors consume part of Claude's context window — even when you're not actively using them.

Only enable the Connectors you're working with right now. Disable the rest between sessions. You'll get more out of each conversation.

03 · Be specific about format and length

Shorter prompts asking for shorter outputs use fewer resources and often produce cleaner results.

Add a length constraint to your prompts: "150 words or fewer," "three bullet points," "one paragraph." You'll get what you need without the filler — and Claude will stop generating the moment it's done.

04 · Use NotebookLM for research, Claude for building

You don't have to do everything in Claude. NotebookLM (Google, free and unlimited) is exceptional for uploading source documents, synthesizing research, and generating summaries.

Use it to do your research and synthesis. Then bring the polished summary into Claude when you're ready to design or build. Two tools, each doing what it does best.

Part Nine · ConnectorsBefore you authorize a Connector.

When you connect Claude to an external tool, you're giving it access to read — and sometimes write — in that tool. That's powerful. It also means it's worth knowing what you're authorizing.

  1. Only enable what you're actively using. Connectors you don't need right now can be disabled. Less access is always safer — re-enable when you're ready to use it.
  2. Check what level of access each Connector requests. Some are read-only (Claude can see your files but not change them). Others are read-write (Claude can also create, edit, or delete). Know the difference before you click Authorize.
  3. For workplace tools — Slack, Google Drive, Gmail — think it through. Ask yourself: am I comfortable with Claude being able to see everything in this account? If the answer is "mostly, but not all of it," look for a way to scope the access, or use a personal account instead.
  4. Turn off Connectors between sessions. You don't need them running in the background when you're not actively working. A quick disable at the end of a session is a good habit.
  5. If something feels off, revoke the access. You can disconnect any Connector at any time from the Connectors settings. No harm done.
Connectors make Claude dramatically more powerful. The caution here isn't about avoiding them — it's about using them intentionally.

Part Ten · GitHubWhat GitHub is. Why it matters. How hard it is.

You don't need to know how to code to use GitHub. You need to know it exists and what it gives you for free.

What it is

A place to store projects and code — think Google Drive, but for websites and apps. It keeps a full version history of everything you've ever saved.

What it gives you

GitHub Pages — free website hosting. Any site you build with Claude Code can go live at a public URL with a few clicks. No monthly hosting fees.

How hard it is

I log on, click a button or two, and leave the rest to learn later. You don't need to be technical to start. Claude Code will walk you through any step you're unsure about.

The three things to know

  1. GitHub is free to sign up. You get free private and public repositories, free version history, and free GitHub Pages hosting. Start at github.com — the signup takes five minutes.
  2. GitHub Pages makes your Artifact shareable without a Claude account. When Claude Code builds an HTML file, you can upload it to GitHub Pages and share the link with anyone — they don't need a Claude account to view it. This is how you get a real standalone deliverable out the door.
  3. Version history is your undo button. Every time you save a change, GitHub keeps the old version. If Claude edits something and breaks it, you can go back. This is the thing developers mean when they say "git." You don't need to understand it fully to benefit from it.
If you're curious about GitHub, ask Claude Code to walk you through setting it up. It will tell you exactly what to do, step by step, and explain the purpose of each step as you go. That's how I learned it.

Appendix · For the curiousGetting started with Claude Code.

This is bonus material. You don't need it to be effective with Claude — but this is how I built the websites, the coloring book generator, and the YouTube player site. My exact setup, honestly: Claude Code as a VS Code extension. No terminal. If I can do this, so can you.

!
Real talk before you start I'm not a developer. I'm an L&D professional who got curious. I haven't used Claude Code for agents, co-work, or anything in the terminal. What follows is my path — not the full product. It's enough to ship something real.

What Claude Code actually is

A version of Claude that lives inside your code editor and can read, write, and edit the files in a folder. You talk to it the same way you talk to the chat — but instead of giving you text back, it changes the files in the project you're working on. For making websites, that's everything.

Before you install: a few things you may need

Depending on what you're building, you may need to install a few helper tools first — things like Git (version control), Node.js (for certain types of websites), or Python (for data-related projects). Don't let that slow you down. Claude Code will tell you exactly what's missing, where to download it, and how to install it. It walks you through every step. You won't need to figure any of it out on your own.

Setup in six steps

  1. Install VS Code. It's free. code.visualstudio.com
  2. Install the Claude Code extension. Search the VS Code Extensions marketplace for "Claude Code." Click install.
  3. Sign in with your Claude account. The extension asks; your free or Pro plan works.
  4. Make a folder for your project. Anywhere on your computer. Open it in VS Code: File › Open Folder.
  5. Open the Claude Code panel. A side panel appears. This is a chat — but it can see and edit every file in your folder.
  6. Describe what you want to build. In plain English. That's it. That's the whole skill.

Your first project

Don't start with a site for a client. Start with a one-page site for yourself. Describe what you want to build and let Claude drive. Here's a prompt that works:

I want to create a personal website that shows my background, the projects I've worked on, and links to ways people can contact me. I want it to look professional and clean, and be easy to update. Walk me through everything I need to do, and explain the purpose of each step as we go. Start by asking me any clarifying questions you need before you build anything.
Tip: Attach a copy of your resume or portfolio in the chat before you start. Give Claude the content it needs to make it yours — not a placeholder. The more specific you are about who you are and what you've done, the better it will look.
Ask Claude to explain why, not just what. Add this to any prompt: "Explain the purpose of each step as you go — I want to understand what we're building, not just follow instructions." This is how you learn while you build. It turns every project into a lesson.

Claude Code will write the files. Double-click the HTML file to open it in your browser. That's your site. Iterate by describing the changes you want: "Make the intro shorter," "swap in this paragraph," "add a softer background color." No technical knowledge required.

A few honest caveats

  • Use is metered. On the Pro plan, Claude Code shares the same message allowance as your chats. You'll hit limits on bigger projects. It's still remarkable.
  • Save your work to GitHub. Ask Claude to set up git and walk you through putting your project on GitHub. This is your undo button — and it's how you get free hosting via GitHub Pages.
  • Read what it writes. You don't need to understand every line — but you should recognize the shape. When something breaks, you'll know where to point.
  • Don't rush to agents. Ship a small thing first. Build confidence before complexity.
If you remember one thing You don't need to know how to code to use Claude Code. You need to know how to describe what you want, read what came back, and ask for changes. If you can write a Project brief, you can do this.
