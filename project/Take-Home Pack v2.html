Part One · 20 promptsThe L&D Prompt Library.

Copy a prompt. Replace the bracketed parts. Run it. Iterate. Organized by ADDIE phase.

Analyze

01 — Learning objectives from a messy transcript

Best for: SME interviews · Output: 3-6 objectives
Act as a senior instructional designer. I'll paste a transcript of an SME interview about [TOPIC]. Generate 3–6 learning objectives written at the appropriate Bloom's level for [AUDIENCE: new hires / managers / etc]. For each objective: state the verb, the content, and the condition. Then briefly flag which parts of the transcript support it. Transcript: [PASTE TRANSCRIPT]

Why it works: forces Claude to ground the objectives in source evidence instead of generating generic ones.

02 — Interrogate a long document

Best for: research, policies, SME docs
I'm attaching a [DOCUMENT TYPE]. Read it carefully. Then, without summarizing yet, answer: 1. What are the 3 most important claims? 2. What assumptions are unstated? 3. Where would a [AUDIENCE] get confused? 4. What 5 questions would I need answered to turn this into a learning experience? [ATTACH / PASTE DOCUMENT]

03 — Compare two frameworks

Best for: competency models, methodologies
I'll paste two [FRAMEWORK TYPE]. Compare them on: - What they agree on - Where they directly conflict - Which audiences each is better suited for - A proposed merged version that preserves the strongest elements of each Framework A: [PASTE A] Framework B: [PASTE B]
Design

04 — Workshop outline from an objective

Best for: instructor-led design · Output: timed table
Design a [DURATION]-minute in-person workshop that achieves this objective: [PASTE OBJECTIVE]. Audience: [AUDIENCE DESCRIPTION + SIZE]. Output a table with columns: time, activity, facilitator action, learner action, and assessment moment. Include at least one activity that surfaces prior experience and one that practices the new behavior. Ask me 3 clarifying questions before you draft.

Why it works: the clarifying-question step prevents generic outputs.

05 — Convert content to a scenario

Best for: moving from lecture to application
Take the policy/procedure below and turn it into a realistic workplace scenario for [AUDIENCE]. The scenario should: - Introduce a dilemma, not just a demonstration - Include 2 distractor details that feel real but aren't decision-critical - End with a decision point, not a conclusion Source: [PASTE POLICY]
Develop

06 — Scenario-based multiple choice

Best for: knowledge + application checks
Generate [N] scenario-based multiple-choice items on [TOPIC] for [AUDIENCE]. Format per item: - Stem: a 2–3 sentence realistic scenario - 4 options (A–D), one correct - A 1-sentence rationale for why each distractor is plausible - The Bloom's level you targeted Avoid trivia. Every item should require judgment.

Why it works: "plausible distractor rationale" is the thing that stops lazy MCQs.

07 — Critique my assessment

Best for: polishing existing items
Here's a set of assessment items I drafted. Critique them as a skeptical assessment specialist would. For each item call out: - Does it test what it claims to test? - Is the stem clear, or does it test reading comprehension? - Are distractors genuinely plausible for a novice? - Any cultural, demographic, or context bias? Be specific. Then rewrite the weakest 3 items. Items: [PASTE ITEMS]

08 — Facilitator script with timing cues

Best for: handoff to other facilitators
Turn the attached outline into a facilitator script for someone delivering this session for the first time. Include: - What the facilitator says (in natural speaking voice) - Timing cues in [brackets] - Watch-outs: where learners commonly get confused - What to do if an activity runs long Outline: [PASTE OUTLINE]

09 — One-page job aid

Best for: reinforcement · Output: printable
Create a one-page job aid for [TASK] that a [ROLE] could keep at their desk. Structure: - Title (6 words max) - 3–5 numbered steps in verb-first voice - A "watch-out" call-out for the most common mistake - An "if X, then Y" decision table for edge cases Optimize for scannability. No paragraphs.

10 — Discussion questions, calibrated

Best for: virtual live, small groups
Generate 6 discussion questions on [TOPIC] calibrated for a [DURATION]-minute small-group conversation. Structure them as: - 2 to surface prior experience (no wrong answers) - 2 to apply a concept to participants' real work - 2 to provoke disagreement For each question, note the Bloom's level and what a facilitator should listen for in the responses.
Implement

11 — Rewrite in my voice

Best for: maintaining consistency across content
Below are 3 writing samples in my voice and a draft that isn't in my voice. Study the samples, identify the moves I make (sentence length, vocabulary, structure, humor, what I avoid), then rewrite the draft to match. Before rewriting, summarize in 5 bullets what you noticed about my voice. I want to see your work. Samples: [PASTE 3 SAMPLES] Draft to rewrite: [PASTE DRAFT]

Why it works: the "show your work" step catches bad voice-matches before you get a full rewrite.

12 — Executive summary for a specific skeptic

Best for: stakeholder buy-in
Summarize the attached [REPORT / PROPOSAL] for a specific reader: [ROLE + WHAT THEY CARE ABOUT + WHAT THEY'RE SKEPTICAL OF]. Output: 150 words or fewer. Lead with the business outcome. Address the skepticism directly. End with the single decision I'm asking them to make. Then flag: what claims in this summary would you verify before sending? [ATTACH / PASTE CONTENT]
Evaluate + Extended ADDIE

13 — Training needs analysis from interview data

Best for: Analyze phase · Output: gap summary + recommendations
I'm going to paste notes from [N] stakeholder or learner interviews about [TOPIC / SKILL AREA]. After reading them all: 1. Identify the top 3 performance gaps across interviewees 2. Note what is causing each gap (knowledge, skill, or environment) 3. Recommend whether training is the right solution for each gap — and if not, what is Be skeptical. Not everything is a training problem. Interview notes: [PASTE NOTES]

Why it works: forces a root-cause lens instead of jumping straight to course design.

14 — Audience gap analysis

Best for: Analyze phase · Output: learner-readiness profile
I need to understand where my audience is starting from before I design this program. Role: [ROLE] Topic: [TOPIC] What I know about them: [PASTE CONTEXT, SURVEYS, PAST DATA] Give me: - What they likely already know (and what I can skip) - Where their common misconceptions or gaps tend to be - What they'll resist or question in this content - One question I should ask before designing anything

15 — Competency map for a role

Best for: Design phase · Output: competency framework draft
Draft a competency map for the role of [ROLE] in a [INDUSTRY / CONTEXT] organization. For each competency: - Name it in plain language (no HR jargon) - Write 2 behavioral indicators at a developing level - Write 2 behavioral indicators at a proficient level Include 4–6 competencies. Flag which ones are most critical to get right in Year 1. Reference: [PASTE JOB DESCRIPTION OR CONTEXT]

16 — Learning experience blueprint

Best for: Design phase · Output: modular course blueprint
I'm designing a [DURATION / FORMAT] program on [TOPIC] for [AUDIENCE]. Build a module-level blueprint with: - 4–6 module titles in learner-facing language - One primary learning objective per module - The recommended modality (ILT, eLearning, job aid, practice, etc.) - A sequencing rationale — why this order Audience context: [PASTE WHAT YOU KNOW ABOUT THEM]

Why it works: separates architecture decisions from content decisions — which is where most design errors happen.

17 — Storyboard draft for one eLearning scene

Best for: Develop phase · Output: scene-by-scene storyboard
Turn the content below into a storyboard for one eLearning scene. Target length: [N] slides or screens. For each screen include: - Screen number and title - Narration script (conversational, no jargon) - On-screen text (max 20 words) - Suggested visual or interaction (describe, don't design) - Learner action, if any Objective for this scene: [OBJECTIVE] Content: [PASTE SOURCE CONTENT]

18 — SME review request email

Best for: Develop phase · Output: review request + feedback guide
Write a short email asking [SME NAME / ROLE] to review the attached [CONTENT TYPE: module / job aid / scenario]. The email should: - Explain what you need reviewed and why their input matters - Give them a clear deadline: [DATE] - Include 4 specific questions to guide their feedback (so you get useful input, not vague edits) - Keep the total ask under 30 minutes of their time Context for the content: [WHAT IT'S FOR AND WHO IT'S FOR]

Why it works: guided feedback questions prevent the "looks great!" response that tells you nothing.

19 — Level 1 evaluation survey

Best for: Evaluate phase · Output: post-session survey
Write a post-session survey for a [DURATION / FORMAT] program on [TOPIC] for [AUDIENCE]. Requirements: - 6–8 questions max - Mix of Likert scale and open-ended - At least one question that identifies what participants will do differently in the next 30 days - One question that surfaces what was missing or unclear Avoid: vanity questions ("Did you enjoy the session?") Focus on: learning + transfer intent

20 — Learning impact summary for stakeholders

Best for: Evaluate phase · Output: ROI-ready brief
I need to summarize the impact of [PROGRAM NAME] for [STAKEHOLDER AUDIENCE]. Data I have: [PASTE EVALUATION DATA, SURVEY RESULTS, OR COMPLETION STATS] Write a one-page impact summary that: - Leads with the business outcome or behavior change - Translates data into plain language (no percentages without context) - Includes one or two direct learner quotes or examples (I'll supply them — leave a placeholder) - Ends with one recommendation for the next iteration Tone: confident, honest, non-defensive about gaps.

Why it works: stakeholders remember stories and recommendations — not completion rates.

Part Two · 7-day challengeOne small thing, every day.

Print this page. Tape it to your monitor. Check a box every day. Seven reps beats one ambitious Saturday.

DAY 01 · Monday

Draft one email with Claude.

Any email. Paste what you know, let Claude draft, then edit. Notice how long it took vs. writing from scratch.

DAY 02 · Tuesday

Interrogate a long doc.

Paste something dense. Ask: "What are 3 questions this raises for a new manager?" Save the questions.

DAY 03 · Wednesday

Generate 5 MCQs. Critique them.

Pick a topic you know cold. Use Prompt 06. Then use Prompt 07 on Claude's own output. Notice what it catches.

DAY 04 · Thursday

Start one Project.

Create a Project for something you're working on this month. Pin one reference file. Write one sentence of instructions.

DAY 05 · Friday

Make one job aid as an Artifact.

Use Prompt 09. Iterate twice. Send it to a colleague for feedback.

DAY 06 · Saturday

Let Claude critique you.

Paste something you wrote last week. Ask: "What's weakest about this? Be direct." Sit with the answer.

DAY 07 · Sunday

Teach one colleague one thing you learned.

Five minutes at the coffee machine. You'll remember it twice as well, and now there are two of you. That's how this gets into your team.

Part Three · Ten habitsSmall moves that compound.

These aren't prompts — they're the posture. Adopt one a week and your outputs will look different in a month.

01
One task per prompt.

If a prompt is doing three jobs, split it. Focused prompts outperform compound ones — every time.

02
Always add success criteria.

"It's good if X, Y, Z." Claude hits what you name. If you don't name it, you get a guess.

03
Front-load context.

Give everything Claude needs up front. Trickling context costs messages and quality.

04
Stop when it's off track.

Don't keep nudging a drifting conversation. Start a new chat with a cleaner prompt.

05
Ask for the method, not the answer.

"Walk me through how you'd approach this" gets you a better answer than "give me the answer."

06
Tell Claude to push back.

"Disagree with me if I'm wrong. Point out what I'm missing." Its default is agreeable — override that.

07
Test before you share.

Run the prompt yourself before passing it to a teammate. Half the time, you'll edit it first.

08
Don't blindly trust outputs.

Especially financial, regulatory, or citation-level detail. Verify anything a stakeholder will read.

09
Iterate in one-sentence edits.

"Shorter." "More skeptical." "Cut the filler." Small moves beat rewriting the whole prompt.

10
Save what works.

Keep a doc of your ten best prompts. Reuse, don't rewrite. This single habit doubles your output.

Part Five · Claude InstructionsTwo templates. Two scopes.

Claude has two places to store standing instructions: Personal Preferences (global, always on) and Project Instructions (scoped to one workspace). Start with Personal Preferences — it's the fastest win.

Personal Preferences / Global Instructions Settings → Personal Preferences

Available on all plans. Applies to every chat you open — inside Projects and outside them. Set your defaults once; you won't repeat yourself again.

[YOUR ROLE] I am a ______________ at ______________. [MY AUDIENCE] Most of what I build is for ______________. [MY DEFAULTS] Unless I say otherwise: • Before you build or draft anything, ask me clarifying questions. I'll say GO when I'm ready. • Write in plain, direct language — no corporate filler. • Use headers and bullets only when they improve clarity. • Don’t offer unsolicited alternatives. • Keep responses under ______________ unless I ask for more. [MY NON-NEGOTIABLES] • Always flag if you’re uncertain about something. • Never cite a source I haven’t given you. • Never give me generic examples — make them specific to my context. • ______________

Where to find it: claude.ai → your profile icon → Settings → Personal Preferences

Project Instructions Inside each Project

Scoped to one body of work. Every chat inside this Project inherits these instructions and your uploaded files automatically.

[ROLE] You are my ______________ for ______________. (e.g., “assessment reviewer for our manager-onboarding redesign”) [AUDIENCE] My learners are ______________. They know ______________ but struggle with ______________. [ALWAYS DO] • Reference [the attached file] before you answer. • Before you draft or build, ask me clarifying questions. I’ll say GO when I’m ready. • ______________ [NEVER DO] • Don’t invent statistics or cite sources I haven’t given you. • Don’t give me generic examples. • ______________ [VOICE] Match the tone of this sample paragraph: "[paste 3–5 sentences you wrote — a real email, a program intro, a learner-facing blurb]" [DEFAULT FORMAT] Unless I say otherwise, give me ______________.

Four places people under-invest

01 · The role

Name the hat. “You are the skeptical SME.” “You are my copy editor.” A named role changes the output more than any other single line.

02 · The voice sample

Paste real writing of yours. 3–5 sentences beats a paragraph of adjectives. If your name goes on it, Claude should sound like you.

03 · The non-negotiables

“Always include an assessment moment.” “Always flag jargon.” House rules, written once, save hours. And tell Claude what NOT to do — equally powerful.

04 · The files

Drop in your style guide, competency model, a learner interview. Name files descriptively — “Q3-manager-sme-interview.docx” is easier for Claude (and you) to reference than “doc1.”

Part Six · The decision guidePrompt vs. Project vs. Skill.

Anything you have to do often should live within Claude — as a Project or a Skill.

If the work is… Use Why
One-off.
I may never do this again.		 A prompt. No overhead. Type it, use it, close the tab.
Recurring, same context.
One client, one course, one workstream.		 A Project. Files, voice, and instructions stay loaded. Every chat inherits them.
Recurring, any context.
A procedure that applies to many topics.		 A Skill. Lives with Claude. Runs automatically when relevant — in any chat, any Project.
Needs another tool.
Canva, Notion, Google Drive, Slack, etc.		 A Connector. Claude reads from / writes to the tool directly. No copy-paste round-trips.
Rule of thumb: Prompts are experiments. Projects are workstreams. Skills are habits. Connectors are bridges.

Part Eight · Work smarterUsing Claude without burning through your plan.

A few habits that stretch your plan further — and improve your results.

01 · The Go Word

Add this to your Personal Preferences: "Before you build or draft anything, ask me clarifying questions. I'll say GO when I'm ready."

This prevents Claude from generating a full output before you've given it all the context — which means you won't spend a message correcting a response that was already wrong. Fewer iterations. Better results on the first try.

02 · Be specific about format and length

Shorter prompts asking for shorter outputs use fewer resources and often produce cleaner results.

Add a length constraint: "150 words or fewer," "three bullet points," "one paragraph." You'll get what you need without the filler.

03 · Use Markdown files to save on usage

What is a Markdown file? A .md file is plain text with simple formatting symbols — # for headings, ** for bold, - for lists. Claude reads and writes Markdown naturally.

Why it saves usage: When you save context as a .md file in your Project knowledge, Claude can reference it precisely without you re-explaining in each chat. Shorter conversations, better responses, fewer wasted messages.

Best for: Instructions, style guides, research notes, outlines, reference material — anything you'd otherwise paste repeatedly. Create one in any text editor and save as filename.md.

04 · Use NotebookLM for research, Claude for building

NotebookLM (Google, free and unlimited) is exceptional for uploading source documents and synthesizing research.

Do your research and synthesis there. Then bring the polished summary into Claude when you're ready to design or build. Two tools, each doing what it does best.

Part Nine · ConnectorsBefore you authorize a Connector.

When you connect Claude to an external tool, you're giving it access to read — and sometimes write — in that tool.

  1. Only enable what you're actively using. Less access is always safer — re-enable when you're ready to use it.
  2. Check what level of access each Connector requests. Some are read-only. Others are read-write (Claude can also create, edit, or delete). Know the difference before you click Authorize.
  3. For workplace tools — Slack, Google Drive, Gmail — think it through. Are you comfortable with Claude being able to see everything in this account? If not, use a personal account or scope the access.
  4. Turn off Connectors between sessions. You don't need them running in the background when you're not actively working.
  5. If something feels off, revoke the access. You can disconnect any Connector at any time from the Connectors settings. No harm done.
Connectors make Claude dramatically more powerful. The caution here isn't about avoiding them — it's about using them intentionally.

Part Ten · GitHubWhat GitHub is. Why it matters. How hard it is.

You don't need to know how to code to use GitHub. You need to know it exists and what it gives you for free.

What it is

A place to store projects and code — think Google Drive, but for websites and apps. It keeps a full version history of everything you've ever saved.

What it gives you

GitHub Pages — free website hosting. Any site you build with Claude can go live at a public URL with a few clicks. No monthly hosting fees.

How hard it is

I log on, click a button or two, and leave the technical stuff to learn later. You don't need to be technical to start.

The three things to know

  1. GitHub is free to sign up. Free private and public repositories, free version history, free GitHub Pages hosting. Start at github.com.
  2. GitHub Pages makes your Artifact shareable without a Claude account. Upload an HTML file and share the link with anyone — they don't need Claude to view it. This is how you get a real standalone deliverable out the door.
  3. Version history is your undo button. Every time you save, GitHub keeps the old version. If something breaks, you can go back.
If you're curious about GitHub, ask Claude to walk you through setting it up. It will tell you exactly what to do, step by step, and explain the purpose of each step as you go.
Hidden gem: GitHub is full of people sharing their ideas, documentation, workflows, and prompts in public repositories — for free. Search GitHub for topics like "instructional design prompts," "Claude workflows," or "L&D AI tools" and you'll find real practitioners sharing what actually works. You can browse, copy, and adapt anything in a public repo. It's one of the most underused learning resources out there.

Part Thirteen · In her own wordsFive Moments That Changed How I See This.

I didn't expect Claude to feel different from other AI tools. These are the five conversations that showed me it was.

Moment 1 — The Redirect
New idea vs. active work

I came in buzzing about a shiny new tool. Claude's response?

"Before you go too far down that rabbit hole — how's Bree's website coming along?"

Then when I showed restraint and just starred the GitHub repo instead of diving in:

"That's actually great impulse control right there. 👏🏾 The system is working."

A collaborator who holds you accountable AND celebrates the small wins? That hit different.

Moment 2 — The Mindset Check
The barrier that wasn't real

I said I couldn't apply to a major opportunity because "I don't have a business yet." Claude didn't agree.

"That's a mindset block worth challenging — not a real barrier. This is exactly the pattern your coach has been working with you on. A massive opportunity shows up, and instead of finding the path in, you find the reason you're not ready yet."

I didn't ask for the truth. I got it anyway.

Moment 3 — The Brain Dump Slowdown
When it tells you to go to bed

I came in with a full download — clients, systems, ideas, family logistics, AT&T tech support drama. All of it, at once. Claude mapped every single thing out, then quietly said:

"Does this give you enough clarity to sleep? Go to bed. We'll pick this up tomorrow. 💙"

I said goodnight. We both meant it.

Moment 4 — The Dreamstorm Debrief
It named what I'd been doing my whole life

After a long late-night session of pure ideation — no building, just wandering — Claude named what I'd been doing my whole life without realizing it had a name.

"You've intuitively developed a healthy practice. You're not 'all over the place' — you're doing legitimate creative work. You just need the follow-through systems."

Then it added:

"Which, ironically, you're really good at building for other people."

...okay, fair.

Moment 5 — The Health Check
The moment I stopped calling it a tool

I mentioned, almost in passing, that I hadn't been taking care of myself — pouring into clients, into Laila, into systems, into everything and everyone but me. Claude didn't let it slide. It paused the whole conversation to name it directly: I was solving for everyone else's capacity while quietly running on empty.

It wasn't harsh. It wasn't a lecture. It was just — seen.

That's the moment I realized this wasn't a tool. It was a thinking partner.

Appendix · For the curiousGetting started with Claude Code.

Bonus material. You don't need it to be effective with Claude — but this is how I built the websites, the coloring book generator, and the YouTube player. My exact setup, honestly described.

!
Real talk before you start I'm not a developer. I'm an L&D professional who got curious. What follows is my path — not the full product. It's enough to ship something real.

Setup in six steps

  1. Install VS Code. It's free. code.visualstudio.com
  2. Install the Claude Code extension. Search the VS Code Extensions marketplace for "Claude Code." Click install.
  3. Sign in with your Claude account. Free or Pro plan works.
  4. Make a folder for your project. Open it in VS Code: File › Open Folder.
  5. Open the Claude Code panel. A side panel appears — it's a chat that can see and edit every file in your folder.
  6. Describe what you want to build. In plain English. That's the whole skill.
If you remember one thing You don't need to know how to code to use Claude Code. You need to know how to describe what you want, read what came back, and ask for changes. If you can write a Project brief, you can do this.
