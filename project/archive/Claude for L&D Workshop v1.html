Skill 07 / 24
The skill

The anatomy of a prompt that works.

  1. Role. Who should Claude be?
    "Act as a senior instructional designer…"
  2. Task. What exactly do you want?
    "Draft a 90-minute workshop outline on…"
  3. Context. Who's it for, what's the goal?
    "Audience is new frontline managers…"
  4. Format. What should the output look like?
    "Give me a table with time, activity, and objective."
  5. Success criteria. How will you know it's good?
    "It's right if every activity names an assessment moment."
[ROLE] Act as a senior instructional designer. [TASK] Draft a 90-minute workshop outline on giving constructive feedback. [CONTEXT] Audience is 20 new frontline managers in a healthcare setting. Half have never led a direct report before. [FORMAT] Table with time, activity, objective, and facilitator cue. [SUCCESS] Every activity names an assessment moment. No lecture longer than 10 minutes. Ask me 3 clarifying questions before you draft.

Patterns 09 / 24
Patterns

Four moves that always sharpen the output.

Pattern 01

Ask for 3 options.

"Give me three versions at different levels of formality." Forces range; makes editing easier.

Pattern 02

Ask it to question you.

"Ask me 5 clarifying questions before you start." Catches assumptions you didn't know you had.

Pattern 03

Show, don't tell.

Paste an example of the voice or format you want. Examples beat adjectives every time.

Pattern 04

Critique, then revise.

"What's weakest about this draft?" — then "rewrite addressing those." Two-step gets you there.

Limits 17 / 24
Honest limits

What Claude gets wrong.

Confident fabrication

It can invent a citation, a statistic, or a quote with total composure. Always verify anything a stakeholder will see.

Generic on autopilot

With thin prompts, you get thin outputs. The quality of your input sets the ceiling.

Lost in long threads

Start a fresh conversation when you pivot topics. Reset context to reset quality.

Out-of-date knowledge

Training data has a cutoff. For anything recent — regulations, tool versions, news — check a current source.

Flattens strong voices

Default output leans tidy and neutral. If you want sharp, funny, or irreverent — you have to ask and show examples.

Over-agrees

It wants to be helpful. Explicitly ask it to push back, disagree, or find holes.

Ethics 18 / 24
Responsible use

Four guardrails for L&D work.

Guardrail 01 — Data

Don't paste what you wouldn't email.

Treat the chat box like an outgoing message. No PII, no salaries, no identifying performance data. Check your company's AI policy; assume one is coming if there isn't.

Guardrail 02 — Attribution

You are the author. You are accountable.

Claude drafts. You decide. Your name goes on the deliverable, and so does the responsibility for what it says.

Guardrail 03 — Disclosure

Be transparent with learners.

When AI helped produce content, say so. Your credibility compounds faster from honesty than from the illusion of effort.

Guardrail 04 — Judgment

Verify before you ship.

Anything a learner will cite — statistics, regulations, case studies — gets checked against a primary source. Every time.

Practical 19 / 24
The free-tier playbook

Four moves to stretch the free plan.

Move 01

Batch your deep work.

Free has daily message limits. Plan a one- or two-hour block, queue up the work you want done, and knock it out in one sitting.

Move 02

Front-load context.

Paste what you need all at once — instead of drip-feeding. You spend fewer messages to get to the same quality of output.

Move 03

Save reusable prompts.

Keep a Google Doc or Notion page of your ten best prompts. Reuse, don't rewrite. This alone doubles your effective output.

Move 04

One big prompt beats ten small ones.

Give the full task with all constraints in one message. Then iterate. You'll spend half the messages for better results.
