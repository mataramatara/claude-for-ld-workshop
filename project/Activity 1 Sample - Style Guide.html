L&D Content Style Guide

Voice, tone, and formatting standards for learning content. Use this as context in your Claude Project so every output matches your house style.

Voice & Tone

Core Voice Principles

Our learning content sounds like a knowledgeable colleague, not a textbook. It's direct without being cold, encouraging without being patronizing, and honest about complexity without hiding behind jargon.

  • Direct. Say what you mean in the first sentence. Don't warm up to the point.
  • Practical. Every concept earns its place by connecting to something learners will actually do.
  • Respectful of time. Say it once, well — don't repeat the same idea in three ways.
  • Honest about difficulty. If something is hard, say so. Learners trust content that acknowledges reality.

Tone by Content Type

  • Facilitator guides: Collegial and confident. Written for someone smart who is doing this for the first time.
  • Learner-facing content: Warm and clear. No corporate speak. Conversational but not casual.
  • Assessment items: Neutral and precise. Every word is deliberate. No hedging language.
  • Job aids: Terse. Verb-first. Imperative. No full paragraphs.
  • Stakeholder communications: Professional, outcome-focused, brief. Lead with business impact.

Language Standards

Words We Use

  • Learner (not "student," "participant," or "trainee")
  • Session (not "training" when referring to a single event)
  • Program (for a multi-module or multi-session body of work)
  • Facilitator (not "trainer" or "instructor")
  • Objective (not "goal" in a formal learning context — reserve "goal" for informal usage)
  • Practice (for activities where learners do something with the content)

Words We Avoid

  • "Utilize" — use "use"
  • "Leverage" as a verb — use "use," "build on," or "apply"
  • "Robust" — be specific about what makes something strong
  • "World-class" or "best-in-class" — unverifiable; avoid
  • "Synergy" — find the actual relationship and name it
  • Passive voice where active is possible: "mistakes were made" → "we made a mistake"

Inclusive Language

  • Use gender-neutral pronouns: "they/them" as a singular default
  • Avoid idioms that don't translate across cultures (e.g., "hit it out of the park," "circle the wagons")
  • Use people-first language for accessibility: "employees with disabilities" not "disabled employees"
  • Scenario names should reflect our workforce demographics — vary names, roles, and contexts

Writing Standards

Learning Objectives

Write at the appropriate Bloom's level. All objectives follow this format: [Audience] will be able to [action verb] [content] [condition/standard].

"By the end of this session, participants will be able to deliver a structured feedback conversation using the SBI model with at least three specific behavioral examples."
  • Use measurable, observable verbs (describe, demonstrate, apply, evaluate) — not vague ones (understand, appreciate, know)
  • One clear behavior per objective — if it takes "and" to connect two behaviors, split them
  • State the condition or standard when it matters for performance

Instructions to Learners

Do this"Read the scenario. Decide what you would say. Be ready to share your reasoning."
Not this"In this next activity, we'd like you to take a look at the scenario that follows and think about what your response might be if you found yourself in this situation."

Sentence Length

Target 15–20 words per sentence in learner-facing content. Complex ideas may need longer sentences — but never longer than 35 words before a period or a dash. Short sentences are not dumbing down. They are respecting attention.

Paragraph Length

In learning content, 3–4 sentences per paragraph is the ceiling. If you can't land the idea in four sentences, break it into a list or split the paragraph. Dense paragraphs are a signal that the idea needs more structure, not more words.

Formatting Standards

Headers

  • Use title case for H1 and H2 headers
  • Use sentence case for H3 and below
  • Never use a header just to break up space — every header should help navigation

Lists

  • Use bulleted lists for unordered items (order doesn't matter)
  • Use numbered lists for sequences, steps, or ranked items
  • Keep list items parallel in structure: all noun phrases, or all verb-first, not mixed
  • Never use a list for fewer than 3 items unless there's a strong visual reason

Emphasis

  • Bold for key terms, names of frameworks, or critical instructions
  • Italics for titles of programs, publications, and light emphasis
  • CAPITALS only for acronyms after first use. Never for emphasis.
  • Avoid underlining — readers will assume it's a link

Assessment Writing Standards

  • Every item tests a learning objective — if you can't name the objective, don't write the item
  • Stems should be complete, meaningful sentences — no fill-in-the-blank stems
  • All four options should be the same part of speech and roughly the same length
  • Avoid "all of the above" and "none of the above"
  • Distractors should be plausible to someone who hasn't learned the content — not trick answers
  • One correct answer per item. If two could be right, the stem needs clarifying
