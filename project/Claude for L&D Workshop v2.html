Prompt moves04 / 21

Five moves that change the output.

The biggest shift isn’t learning prompts — it’s treating Claude as a thinking partner, not just an executor. These five moves are how you hold up your end of that conversation.

  1. Ask it to ask you.
    “Before you draft, ask me 3 clarifying questions.” Claude’s best feature is its questions.
  2. Name success criteria.
    “It’s good if every activity names an assessment moment.” Claude hits what you name.
  3. Ask for pushback.
    “Disagree with me where I’m wrong. Point out what I’m missing.” Overrides its default agreeableness.
  4. Critique, then revise.
    “What’s weakest about this draft?” → “Now rewrite fixing those.” Two-step beats one-shot.
  5. One task per prompt.
    Focused prompts outperform compound ones. If you’re asking for more than one thing, split it.
[ROLE] Senior instructional designer. [TASK] Draft a 90-minute workshop outline on giving constructive feedback. [AUDIENCE] 20 new frontline managers in a healthcare setting. Half have never led a direct report. [SUCCESS] Every activity names an assessment moment. No lecture block longer than 10 minutes. [FORMAT] Table: time · activity · objective · facilitator cue. Before you draft, ask me 3 questions. Push back where my scope feels off.

Act Two · Projects06 / 21
Stop re-explaining yourself every chat

Projects: Claude’s memory for your work.

A Project is a container. Drop in reference files, write instructions once, and every conversation inside starts with all of it already loaded.

Context vs. Memory Context is what Claude can see right now — in the current conversation window. Memory (via Projects) is what persists across sessions: your files, your instructions, your voice. Context resets when you close a chat. Memory doesn’t.
  • One Project per client, course, or recurring workstream
  • Attach your style guide, competency model, SME transcripts, assessment results, learner interviews, industry research, meeting notes, writing samples
  • Every chat inside inherits instructions & context
  • Name files descriptively — “Q3-manager-sme-interview.docx” is easier to reference than “doc1.docx”
  • Come back a month later — Claude remembers

Best Practice07 / 21
Instructions are where most people under-invest

There are two types of instructions. Both matter.

Claude Instructions
Personal Preferences / Global Instructions

Applies to every chat — inside Projects and outside them

Project Instructions Inside each Project

Scoped to one workspace — inherits global defaults

Describe the role

“You are my assessment reviewer.” “You are the skeptical SME.” Name the hat — it shapes everything that follows.

Name the non-negotiables

“Always flag jargon.” “Always include a learner persona.” And just as important: tell Claude what NOT to do. “Never cite sources I haven’t given you. Never give me generic examples.”

Show your voice

Paste a paragraph you wrote. 3–5 sentences beats a dozen adjectives. If your name goes on it, Claude should sound like you.

Paste your reference files

Style guide, competency model, one learner interview. Every chat in this Project inherits them. You stop re-explaining yourself.

Before You Build09 / 21
Two settings worth setting before your first chat

Tell Claude who you are — once.

Personal Preferences / Global Instructions

Your global defaults.

Settings → Personal Preferences. Applies to every chat — not just Projects. Set your role, your preferred format, and how you like to work. Write it once; it’s always there.

Project Instructions

Scoped to one body of work.

Global = always on. Project = scoped to that workspace. Together, Claude starts every conversation knowing who you are and what you’re building — without you repeating yourself.

The Go Word Add this to your Personal Preferences: “Before you build or draft anything, ask me clarifying questions. I’ll say GO when I’m ready.” — This one line stops Claude from generating before you’ve given it all the context. Better output, fewer do-overs.
TIP 01

Use NotebookLM (free, unlimited) for research and source synthesis first — bring the polished summary to Claude when you’re ready to design or build.

TIP 02

Be specific about output format and length: “150 words or fewer,” “a table,” “three bullet points.” Shorter instructions produce cleaner results.

Artifacts: think, then ship.

An Artifact is a real, working thing — not a description of a thing. It appears in a side panel: a document, a web page, a live quiz, a small app. Built inside the chat. Ready to share the moment Claude finishes.

  • Not a draft to clean up later — a thing you can use today
  • Web pages & interactive widgets
  • Quizzes, games, decision trees
  • Live previews you can click and iterate on
Important to know Claude builds the front end (the interactive experience). The back end (saving scores, LMS integration) requires a developer or tool like Zapier. Sharing an Artifact also requires the recipient to have a Claude account.

What Claude gets wrong.

🚫

📅

Guardrails18 / 21
Three guardrails, non-negotiable

Responsible use, in L&D.

01 · Data

Don’t paste what you wouldn’t email.

  • No PII. No salaries. No identifying performance data.
  • Treat the chat box like an outgoing message to a vendor.
  • Check your AI policy — assume one is coming if it isn’t.
02 · Attribution

You are the author.

  • Claude drafts. You decide.
  • Your name goes on the deliverable — and the responsibility.
  • Be transparent with learners when AI helped produce content.
03 · Judgment

Verify before you ship.

  • Anything a learner will cite — stats, regulations, case studies — check it against a primary source.
  • Every time. No exceptions.
  • Also test Artifacts before sharing. They sometimes have bugs.
