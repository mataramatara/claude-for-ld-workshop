Prompt moves05 / 24
Not a prompt alphabet — five moves that punch above their weight

Five moves that change the output.

The biggest shift isn’t learning prompts — it’s treating Claude as a thinking partner, not just an executor. These five moves are how you hold up your end of that conversation.

  1. Ask it to ask you.
    “Before you draft, ask me 3 clarifying questions.” Claude’s best feature is its questions.
  2. Name success criteria.
    “It’s good if every activity names an assessment moment.” Claude hits what you name.
  3. Ask for pushback.
    “Disagree with me where I’m wrong. Point out what I’m missing.” Overrides its default agreeableness.
  4. Critique, then revise.
    “What’s weakest about this draft?” → “Now rewrite fixing those.” Two-step beats one-shot.
  5. One task per prompt.
    Focused prompts outperform compound ones. If you’re asking for more than one thing, split it.
[ROLE] Senior instructional designer. [TASK] Draft a 90-minute workshop outline on giving constructive feedback. [AUDIENCE] 20 new frontline managers in a healthcare setting. Half have never led a direct report. [SUCCESS] Every activity names an assessment moment. No lecture block longer than 10 minutes. [FORMAT] Table: time · activity · objective · facilitator cue. Before you draft, ask me 3 questions. Push back where my scope feels off.

Best Practice08 / 24
Instructions are where most people under-invest

Writing instructions that actually stick.

Treat it like an SOP

Not a spell — a standing order.

If a new teammate joined your project tomorrow, what would you tell them on Day 1? That’s what goes here. Concrete, specific, opinionated.

Describe the role

Who is Claude, for you?

“You are my assessment reviewer.” “You are the skeptical SME.” Name the hat — it shapes everything.

Name the non-negotiables

What should always happen?

“Always flag jargon.” “Always include a learner persona.” These are the house rules. Write them once, save hours.

Show your voice

Paste a paragraph you wrote.

“Match the voice of this sample.” A short real example beats a dozen adjectives.

Before You Build10 / 24
Two settings worth knowing before your first chat

Tell Claude who you are — once.

Personal Instructions

Your global defaults.

Settings → Instructions. Applies to every chat — not just Projects. Set your role, your preferred format, and how you like to work. Write it once; it’s always there.

Project Instructions

Scoped to one body of work.

Personal = always on. Project = scoped to that workspace. Together, they mean Claude starts every conversation knowing who you are and what you’re building — without you repeating yourself.

The Go Word Start any session with: “Don’t build anything yet. Ask me 3 clarifying questions first. I’ll say GO when I’m ready.” — Stops Claude from generating before you’ve given it all the context. Saves tokens, saves back-and-forth, gets better output on the first try.
USAGE TIP 01

Turn off Connectors you aren’t actively using — they consume context.

USAGE TIP 02

Use NotebookLM for research first — bring the summary to Claude when you’re ready to build.

USAGE TIP 03

Be specific about output format and length — shorter instructions = fewer tokens used.

Artifacts: think, then ship.

An Artifact is a real, working thing — not a description of a thing. It appears in a side panel: a document, a web page, a live quiz, a small app. Built inside the chat. Ready to share the moment Claude finishes.

  • Not a draft to clean up later — a thing you can use today
  • Web pages & interactive widgets
  • Quizzes, games, decision trees
  • Live previews you can click and edit
Important to know What Claude builds runs in the browser — no setup required. Front-end = the interactive experience (quiz, sim, widget). Back-end = saving scores, tracking data, connecting to your LMS. Claude builds the front end. For the back end, you’d need a developer or a tool like Zapier. Sharing a conversation sim also requires the recipient to have a Claude account.

Real Talk19 / 24
The parts they don't put in the ad

What Claude gets wrong.

01

Citations & specifics.

It will invent a plausible-sounding source. Verify every stat, every study, every regulation against a primary source.

02

Your context.

It doesn’t know your org, your learners, or your politics. You have to tell it. Every time, until you save it.

03

Recency.

Its training has a cutoff. For anything that changed this quarter, paste the source in — don’t ask it to remember.

04

Deep subject-matter judgment.

It drafts like a smart generalist. For nuance — compliance, clinical, legal — you’re still the SME.

05

Agreeableness.

Default mode is “helpful.” Tell it to push back, or it’ll validate a wobbly idea.

06

Your learners.

It models a generic professional. A real learner is messier, more specific, and more interesting. Keep them in the loop.

Guardrails20 / 24
Three guardrails, non-negotiable

Responsible use, in L&D.

01 · Data

Don’t paste what you wouldn’t email.

No PII. No salaries. No identifying performance data. Treat the chat box like an outgoing message to a vendor. Check your AI policy — assume one is coming if there isn’t.

02 · Attribution

You are the author.

Claude drafts. You decide. Your name goes on the deliverable — and so does the responsibility for what it says. Be transparent with learners when AI helped produce content.

03 · Judgment

Verify before you ship.

Anything a learner will cite — statistics, regulations, case studies — gets checked against a primary source. Every time. No exceptions.
